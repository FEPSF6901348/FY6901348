Introduction

Time Crystals are a part of condensed matter physics. Time Crystals are a quantum system of particles whose lowest energy state is one in which particles are in repetitive motion. The system cannot lose energy to the environment and come to rest because it is already in its quantum ground state. Time Crystals are relatively new and exotic phase of matter that was proposed by Nobel laureate Frank Wilczek in 2012 [1].

1.1 Time Translation Symmetry

Time translation symmetry (also known as temporal translation symmetry). It is a fundamental concept in physics and maths. It refers to the invariance of physical laws and phenomena over time. In other words, if a physical system exhibits time translation symmetry, it means that the laws governing that system remain unchanged as time progresses.

1.1.1 Conservation of Energy

Time translation symmetry is closely related to the conservation of energy. If the laws of physics do not change over time, it implies that the total energy of a closed physical system remains constant over time. This principle is known as the conservation of energy. When physicists derive conservation of energy in a system, they start by considering time translation symmetry. By analysing the equations of motion, they can show that the total energy remains constant because the equations for kinetic and potential energy do not change with time.

1.1.2 Mathematical Formulation and Broken Symmetry

Mathematically, time translation symmetry is often expressed using the concept of time derivatives. If the laws of physics are invariant under time translations, it means that the equations describing the system's behaviour remain the same when time is shifted by a constant value. If you have a physical system described by equations and you have the variable t (time) with t + change in t where change in t is constant.

Many physical systems exhibit TTS (time translation symmetry). For instance: Laws of motion in classical mechanics, Quantum Mechanics using Schrödinger's equation. Broken Symmetry is when TTS can be broken, for example in the presence of external forces or non-conservative processes, the total energy of a system may not remain constant. This would lead to a violation of TTS.

1.2 Quantum Computing

Quantum computers represent a completely new approach to computing. By using principles of quantum physics, they are able to solve very complex statistical problems that normal computers can't. Quantum Computing is built on quantum bits, or qubits, which can store zeros and ones. Qubits can represent any combination of both zero and one simultaneously. This is known as superposition [2].

1.2.1 Time Crystals in Quantum Computing

Google's Quantum Computing team made headlines in 2019 when they performed the first-ever computation that ordinary computers weren't thought to be able to do in a practical amount of time. The time crystal demo marks one of the first times a Quantum Computer has found gainful employment. Google's qubits consist of superconducting aluminium strips. Each has two possible energy states, which can be programmed to represent spins pointing up or down. For the demo collaborators used a chip with 20 qubits to serve as the time crystal [3].

1.3 'Recipe' of Time Crystals

A Time Crystal is a newly realised phase of matter in which particles move in a regular, repeating cycle without burning any energy. The phase arises through a combination of three special ingredients.

A row of particles, each with a magnetic orientation, or 'spin', will ordinarily settle into an arrangement with the lowest possible energy but random interference can make the particles get stuck in a higher-energy configuration. The effect is called Many-Body Localisation.

Many-body localised systems can exhibit a special kind of order: if you flip all the spins in the system you get another Many-body localised state. This is known as the Eigenstate Order.

Finally, the last 'ingredient' needed is known as a Periodic Driver. If you drive the system with a laser, it will forever cycle (flip) between the states without absorbing any net energy from the laser. This has now formed a Time Crystal.

1.4 Conclusion

Our effort to realize a time crystalline phase uses quantum processors in a unique way. That’s because computations are usually carried out on these processors with a set of logical operations called quantum gates. The computation has no relation to the underlying governing dynamics of the system – in technical terms, the desired Hamiltonian is not realized on the processor. In such computations, the processor is used in roughly the same way as in a classical computer.

However, this quest of establishing a dynamical phase is fundamentally different. Here, the question is whether a stable phase can emerge in a many-body driven isolated system. For such questions, our processor and our setting is indeed the natural platform to explore such questions. Our implementation is on the direct path toward realization of this dynamical phase. The challenge that makes our result fall short of a full realization is extrapolating the response to infinite time and system size, which are all ultimately rooted in the finite coherence time of the system. The core of our work and achievements is to devise experimental methods to provide a basis for making such extrapolations.

As our results show, the protocols developed here are general and establish a scalable approach to studying non-equilibrium phases of matter on NISQ processors [4].
